suprasegmentals.md


  • What does suprasegmental mean?

    Suprasegmentals are linguistic features that go beyond the individual sounds.
      - Intonation/ stress
      - Linking of sounds

  • What are silent letters in English?

    Silent r
      In non-rhotic (British) English, unless we have a case of linking r, the final r of a word is not pronounced.
      "water" is said like "watuh"
      "fever" is said like "fevah"
      "perfect" is said like "puhfect"
    .
    Silent s
      "island" is said like "iland"
    .
    Silent plosives
      p ("pterydactyl" like "terydactyl")
      b ("bomb" like "bom")
      t ("match" like "mach")
      d ("sandwich" like "sanwich")
      k ("knob" like "nob")
      g ("design" like "desine)
    .
    Silent h
      "honest" is said like "onest"
      "hour" is said like "our"
    .
    Silent w
      "answer" is said like "anser"
      "whole" is said like "hole"

  • Describe the process of linking. When does it occur most often?

    Typical feature of the English language, in contrast to German, where there is a glottal stop. between. every. word, In english, words can become connected and thereby pronounced more smoothly.
    .
    Typically occurs as Consonant-to-vowel linking: The first word ends with a constonant, the next begins in a vowel.
    Example:
      "an apple" is said like "anapple"
      "hold on" is said like "whole-don"
    .
    (Also happens by use of the linking r)

  • What is the linking r?

    When one word ends in an /r/ sound and the next begins with a vowel, the words are linked.
    Example:
      "for a minute" is said like "fora minute"
      "far away" is said like "faraway"

  • What is the intrusive r?

    Though no 'r' is written, one is added between two words in pronunciation for smoothness.
    Example:
      "idea of" is said like "idearof"
      "law and order" is said like "lawrand order"

  • What is elision?

    Sounds disappear from speech, for smoother pronunciation.
    .
    Initiial /h/
      "with him" is said like "with'im"
    .
    Final /d/ and /t/
      "next day" is said like "nex'day"
      "must go" is said like "mus'go"

  • What are weak and strong forms? Give examples

    Structure-words (non-content words) (i.e. most pronouns, auxilary verbs (helper verbs, without content) and structure words (prepositions, conjunctions, articles)) can be...
    - Strong, if...
      - said in isolation
      - stressed for emphasis ("It was MY fault!")
      - sometimes initial position, and mostly in final position ("What are you waiting FOR?")
    .
    - Weak, in all other cases!
    -> Sounds change, some constonants are dropped.
    .
    /ɪ/ instead of /iː/
      Weak: "She is very nice"
      Strong: "Shi is very nice"
    .
    /ʊ/ instead of /u:/ 
      Weak: "How are juh?"
      Strong "How are youᵘ?"
    .
    /ə/ for other vowels
      Weak: "Ah you coming?"
      Strong: "Arr you coming?"
