  • Name some of the main differences between British and American English!

    British
    - Non-rhotic
    - More rapid, eliminates some vowels
    - Post-alveolar approximant [r] ("bIrd")
    .
    American     
    - Rhotic (always pronounces the 'r')
    - Feels slower/drawled
    - Post-alveolar approximant: [ɹ] ("buhrd")

  • What is rhoticity?

    Rhoticity describes whether or not the /r/ is preserved in all phonetic environments.
    .
    British English (being non-rhotic) does not always pronounce the 'r', in particular when it comes after a vowel
    Example:
      "far from" is said like "fa' from"
      "water" is said like "watuh"
      "perfect" is said like "puhfect"
    .
    (i.e.: the final r is skipped, except when it is a linking r (when the next word begins in a vowel, like "for us" -> "forus")

  • Why is American English called a rhotic language?

    Because in American English, the 'r' is pronounced in all phonetic environments.

  • What is the post-alveolar approximant [ɹ] in AE?

    Bird is pronounced "buhrd".
    The r-sound is darker.

  • What is the post-alveolar approximant [r] in BrE?

    Bird is pronounced "bIrd".
    The r-sound is lighter.

  • When is the /r/ pronounced in BrE?

    - between two vowels (carrot)
    - start of a word (read)
    - linking r

  • What are the three variations of the [r] in BrE?

    - flapped /r/
      (when a king says "verry")
    - linking /r/
      ("for a minute" -> "fora minute")
    - intrusive /r/
      ("idea of" -> "idearof")

  • What is the flapped /r/?

    One roll of a rolled r (so you roll the r, but only shortly)
    Example:
      When a king says "verry"
    .
    (Other languages, like spanish, use this lot)

  • What is the linking r?

    When one word ends in an /r/ sound and the next begins with a vowel, the words are linked.
    Example:
      "for a minute" is said like "fora minute"
      "far away" is said like "faraway"

  • Explain what the intrusive r is. Give an example.

    Though no 'r' is written, one is added between two words in pronunciation for smoothness.
    Example:
      "idea of" is said like "idearof"
      "law and order" is said like "lawrand order"

  • What are centring diphthongs?

    Diphthongs are glides from one pure vowel into an adjecent one. Like in "hound"
    .
    Centering diphtongs specifically end in an /ə/ (which is a central vowel)
    Example:
      beer -> beah bɪə(r)
      sure -> shuah ʃʊə
      where -> wheah weə

  • What is t flapping? Which variety of English uses this?

    A /t/ or /d/ (plosive sounds) can become non-plosive for smoother pronunciation.
    "water" becomes "wadder"
    (your tongue only taps against the roof of your mouth, rather than building up pressure)
    .
    British uses this.
    Brtish:  "Wadduh"
    American: "WaTer"
